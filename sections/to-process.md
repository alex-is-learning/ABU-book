## Terms

**A** — "true & useful, AND new to me" / "this wasn't in my world model before, but it is now"

**B** — "true & useful, but NOT novel" / "this is already in my world model"

**U** — "unclear, undefined, unknowable, or untrue" / "not in my world model, but I am not going to add it" *(regardless of whether it's true or not, it has no relevance or useful predictive power to you)*

**A ceiling** — your frontier

**B floor** — a minimum set of beliefs / knowledge you share with someone (also: minimum personal prerequisite knowledge to understand something)

### Reminder of the A/B/U model

![](images/A_B_U_gifs.png)

The thing I learned a few days ago is that "A"s actually mature into "B"s when you test them and they seem to hold up!! This is not captured in their current onboarding page!!

- An "A" is a new and seemingly correct belief/idea, that slots into your model
- A "B" is a more mature A: you've tested it and it now seems like a tested, provisional truth

### Key (for gifs below)

- Red nodes are "A"s (brand new, untested, but seem true)
- Green nodes are "B"s (have been tested, appear to be true)
- Purple, "outsider" nodes are "U"s — new bits of data, new ideas that are unknown. Are they true? Are they bullshit? Currently, you're unsure.

### Gaining As, A frontier

![](images/gif1-frontier-growth.gif)

### An A maturing to B

An A matures into a B when it receives some data from the outside world that confirms it. An A is an untested belief; a B is a belief that now has some empirical, from-the-universe data to support it.

One example: you test the A in the real world and find that it empowers you, or is predictive. E.g. "oh shit, ok, I think that person is feeling x way because of y, I didn't know y could make you feel x!" (an A, "it seems that y can make you feel x") → ask them and they confirm → now it's a B.

Vs, you assume that, you act on it ("oh, they must be feeling x because of y, so I'll take this action to address y") → they say "dude wtf, you were totally wrong, you misread the situation" → oh damn ok, that A was wrong.

## Maladaptive Bs

### One of your "B"s leads to an action that burns someone

A "B", a belief you have, burns someone.

*(The whole write-up is inspired by my relatively new "it's very very important that I say things really honestly even if it comes across as disagreeable, sugar coating is counterproductive and a waste of time!!" burning a coworker.)*

![](images/mal1-maladaptive-b-fires.gif)

### The burned person sends data your way

"Hey, wtf, that sucked!" — and they may offer some advice (or a mentor does), e.g. "look, even if you have a good point, it's much better to communicate it skillfully, and ask questions, don't come at this from a place of 'this belief of mine is definitely true, and I'm stressed about the implications'".

But, this advice lands for you as a "U" — it clashes too much with your B ("I must be honest no matter what!!" + maybe another one "I can't waste time on things like sugar coating!!!").

You already have locked-in beliefs that are hard to change, that are Resistant to Change (*a book by Robert Kegan about this!*), so their advice remains a "U" and fades away.

![](images/mal2-feedback-rejected.gif)

### Alternative: their feedback lands as an "A", and matures to a "B"

You have the scaffolding/prerequisites required for their feedback to land as an "A" — maybe they deliver the feedback gently/patiently and scaffold you to a place where you can get it. Or maybe it's advice that you were already *close* to getting anyway, as it's near your "A frontier".

And then, you consolidate it to a "B", and it counterbalances the original "B" that causes the pain/the maladaptive behaviour.

In this version, the new B ends up around the same size/weight as the old. They're in orbit together, counterbalancing each other.

![](images/mal3-feedback-integrated-equal.gif)

### Same as above but heavier weight

In this version, the new B weighs much more than the old B. An echo of the old belief remains, but it has been heavily outweighed by this new one. Maybe you finally realise that that old B was useful for you up until the age of like, 10, but has been super maladaptive since then — you've just never foregrounded this fact or realised that there is an alternative.

## Robert Kegan — "Immunity to Change"

*Summary of the Immunity Map (via NotebookLM)*

The **Immunity Map** is a diagnostic tool — a mental "X-ray" — designed to make visible the hidden internal dynamics that prevent individuals and groups from achieving their most desired changes. It reveals a "dynamic equilibrium" where a person has "one foot on the gas" (the sincere desire to change) and "one foot on the brake" (the self-protective system keeping the status quo).

The map has four columns:

**Column 1: Commitment (Improvement Goal)** — A specific, high-leverage improvement goal that the person is genuinely committed to. A good goal should be *adaptive* — meaning it requires the person to grow — rather than a technical skill that can be easily learned.

**Column 2: Fearless Inventory** — Concrete behaviors currently engaged in (or not done) that work *against* the Column 1 goal. Not for problem-solving or explaining *why* the behaviors happen, but for descriptive honesty about how one is currently undermining their own aspirations.

**Column 3: Hidden Competing Commitments** — The heart of the immunity. Begins with the "Worry Box": identify the fears that arise when imagining doing the opposite of the behaviors in Column 2. These fears become *competing commitments* — active, self-protective motivations to ensure the scary things do not happen. These make the obstructive behaviors in Column 2 look perfectly sensible from a self-protection standpoint.

**Column 4: Big Assumptions** — The core beliefs or mental models that sustain the entire immune system. Called "Big Assumptions" because they are taken as incontrovertible truths rather than possibilities. They create the "Danger Zone" that makes the self-protection in Column 3 feel necessary.

**The purpose:** help a person move a mental structure from *subject* (something that "has" them) to *object* (something they can look at, test, and control). By converting the contradiction a person *is* into a contradiction they *have*, they can begin to disrupt the immune system through **SMART tests** — safe, modest, actionable experiments designed to challenge the validity of their Big Assumptions.
